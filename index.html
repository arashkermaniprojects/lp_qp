What the pieces in the banner mean

The dashed green lines are iso-value lines (also called level lines or level sets). Every point that lies on a single dashed line gives the exact same total value of the objective — the same profit, the same cost, the same score, whatever quantity you are optimizing. Parallel dashed lines just mean different total values; moving from one line to the next changes the objective by a fixed amount.

The green "better →" arrow points in the direction that increases the objective, and it is always perpendicular to the level lines. If your goal is to maximize profit, then "better" literally means more profit per unit step in that direction. (If the problem were a minimize — say, cost — the arrow would flip around: "better" would point toward lower cost.) The bold green sweep line in the animation moves in this direction; it keeps going until it just barely touches the polygon and cannot advance any further while staying inside. That last-touched point is the optimum.

So the arrow is there to answer the question "which way is better?" — without it, you could not tell whether the dashed lines are showing profit going up or going down.

1 · What is Linear Programming?

Linear programming (LP) is the art of making the best decision when everything — the goal you care about, and the rules you must obey — is linear in the quantities you control.

"Linear" here is the entire story. If doubling an input doubles the output, and two combined choices add up the way you'd expect, you are in LP territory. That covers an astonishing amount of real-world planning: what to produce, what to ship, what to schedule, what to blend.

Key idea. Choose numbers x1, x2, … to maximize or minimize a linear combination of them, subject to linear inequality constraints. That's all an LP is.

The whole field exists because this special shape of problem — linear in, linear out — can be solved very fast, even when there are millions of variables. Every operations research textbook starts here for that reason.

2 · Anatomy of an LP

Every LP has three parts:

  1. Decision variables — the knobs you control (how many chairs to make, how much to ship on each route).
  2. Objective — one linear function of those variables you want to make as large or as small as possible.
  3. Constraints — linear inequalities the variables must satisfy (budgets, capacities, minimums).

In symbols, a standard LP looks like this:

maximize    c1·x1 + c2·x2 + … + cn·xn
subject to   ai1·x1 + ai2·x2 + … + ain·xn  ≤  bi   for every constraint i
            xj ≥ 0    (usually)

The last line — variables being non-negative — is a convention rather than a requirement. "You can't ship −5 units" makes that constraint natural.

How to read the factory plot

The axes are real quantities. Horizontal = chairs you decide to make; vertical = tables. Every point in the first quadrant is one specific production plan — "make 5 chairs and 3 tables" sits at (5, 3).

The plot shows three different families of lines, and they mean different things. Colors are your friend here:

  • Dashed blue line — the wood limit. Each chair uses 1 unit of wood, each table uses 3. With W wood available, a plan must satisfy 1·chairs + 3·tables ≤ W. Endpoints: (W, 0) on the chairs axis and (0, W/3) on the tables axis. These are not at equal values, because each product consumes a different amount of wood.
  • Dashed orange line — the labor limit. Each chair uses 2 hours, each table uses 2 hours — equal rates. With H hours the rule is 2·chairs + 2·tables ≤ H, so both intercepts sit at H/2. Slide Labor Hours from 16 up to 19 and the endpoints move from (8, 0) and (0, 8) to (9.5, 0) and (0, 9.5) — the whole line slides outward symmetrically.
  • Solid green line — the profit line (not a constraint!). It is a level set of the objective: every plan on this line earns the same total profit 40·chairs + 50·tables. Its slope is −(profit per chair) / (profit per table), which is generally not symmetric — so its x-intercept and y-intercept are different numbers (in the screenshot: x ≈ 8.5, y ≈ 6.8). That is not a paradox; the green line is the objective, not the labor constraint. The animation sweeps this line outward until it last touches the polygon — that touch point is the optimum.

4 · Geometry: polytope and level lines

Every LP has the same geometric picture, regardless of the story on top:

  • The constraints carve out a feasible region: a polygon in 2D, a polyhedron in higher dimensions. It always has flat faces and sharp corners.
  • The objective is a linear function, whose level sets are parallel lines (in 2D), parallel planes (in 3D), parallel hyperplanes in general.
  • Optimizing means pushing the level line as far as possible in the good direction while staying inside the region.
The corner theorem. If an LP has a finite optimum, at least one optimal point sits at a corner (vertex) of the feasible region. This is why LP algorithms search corners, not interiors.

The intuition is simple: a straight ramp leans against a flat-sided box. The highest point you can touch on the ramp is a corner of the box. The only exception is when the ramp is exactly parallel to one face — then a whole edge ties for first place, but a corner is still among the winners.

5 · How solvers find the answer

Since the optimum lives at a corner, a solver only has to hunt among corners. The Simplex method, invented by George Dantzig in 1947, does exactly that: start at any corner, walk to an adjacent corner that improves the objective, repeat until no neighbor is better.

In practice, modern LP solvers also use interior-point methods, which cut through the inside of the polytope instead of walking its corners. For gigantic problems they are sometimes faster. Both families are taught together in a dedicated optimization course; for us today, the corner intuition is enough.

6 · Where LP shows up

A tiny sample of the things LP is quietly running:

  • Airline crew scheduling — assign pilots and flight attendants to flights, minimizing cost while meeting rest, qualification, and home-base rules.
  • Supply chain planning — decide how much of each product to make at each factory and ship through which warehouse to which region.
  • Portfolio construction (linear version) — maximize expected return subject to a total-weight budget and per-asset caps.
  • Network flow & routing — push as much data, oil, or electricity through a network as its pipe capacities allow.
  • Diet & blending — the original 1940s application: cheapest mix of foods meeting nutritional requirements.

The unifying pattern: a goal that adds up linearly, constraints that are "no more than X" or "at least Y", and variables that can be any non-negative amount. If your problem fits that shape, you have a linear program.

7 · Takeaways

  • An LP is: linear objective + linear inequality constraints + usually non-negative variables.
  • The feasible set is a polytope — flat faces, sharp corners.
  • The optimum, if finite, sits at a corner. Solvers exploit this.
  • Simplex walks corner-to-corner. Interior-point methods cut through the middle. Both work well.
  • LP is the workhorse of operational planning. Recognizing when a problem is an LP is the hard part — the solving is done for you.

1 · Beyond linear costs

Linear objectives are a clean starting point, but many real goals don't stay linear — they curve. Being off-target usually hurts faster than proportionally: missing by 2 is more than twice as bad as missing by 1. In most real settings, mistakes are penalized not by how far off you are, but by how far off you are squared.

Three everyday settings where this "quadratic penalty" shows up naturally:

  • Tracking or control. A thermostat that is 1° off its setpoint is fine; a thermostat that is 10° off is not ten times worse, it is much worse than that. Comfort, energy waste, and corrective effort all scale badly with big deviations, so the cost of being off is naturally written as (deviation)² rather than |deviation|.
  • Financial risk. In finance, "risk" usually means variance — how spread out the possible future outcomes are. If you put twice as much money into a volatile asset (what finance people call doubling your "exposure"), the variance of your final wealth roughly quadruples, not just doubles: it grows with the square of the amount invested. So minimizing risk is a naturally quadratic objective.
  • Fitting a line to data. When you draw a best-fit line through a scatter of points, each point's "error" is its vertical distance from the line. The standard rule — least squares — scores each error by its square, so a point that is 2 units off the line counts four times as much as a point 1 unit off. This is why drawing a straight line through noisy data is a quadratic problem.
Why the power of 2 — not 1, not 3?

Fair question. There is nothing magical about the number 2, but it is the sweet spot between three competing pressures:

  • Power 1 (just |mistake|) treats every unit of error equally — a mistake of 10 is exactly ten times worse than a mistake of 1. It does not punish big mistakes any extra, and it has a sharp kink at zero that makes smooth optimization awkward (the derivative is undefined there). It is occasionally used in "robust" statistics when you specifically want to tolerate outliers, but it does not give you a smooth bowl.
  • Power 3 or higher punishes large mistakes enormously but almost ignores small ones — usually not what real goals look like. It also breaks the clean "linear gradient, constant curvature" structure that makes quadratic problems solvable quickly and in closed form. You lose the algorithms without gaining useful behavior.
  • Power 2 is what every smooth cost function actually looks like near its minimum. This is the deepest reason, and it comes from Taylor's theorem: if you zoom in on the minimum of any smooth function, the linear (1st-order) term vanishes — a minimum is flat — and the quadratic (2nd-order) term is the first piece that is not zero. So "squared" is not an arbitrary modeling choice: it is literally the local shape of every smooth cost, no matter how complicated the cost is globally.

On top of that intrinsic reason, power 2 plays very nicely with familiar geometry — Pythagoras a² + b² = c², the length of a vector, the definition of variance, the notion of projection onto a line — all of these are sums of squares. And it keeps the math tidy: the gradient of a quadratic is linear, which is exactly the ingredient fast solvers need. Any other power breaks at least one of these three things — natural local shape, natural geometry, natural solvability — and you rarely gain anything in return.

So — is the squared cost nature, or our decision?

Honest answer: both, depending on which quantity you are looking at. It is worth being explicit about the difference.

  • It is nature when the square is forced on us by a definition or a law. Variance has to be defined with a square — remove the square and the variances of independent random things stop adding up, the whole of statistics falls apart. Euclidean distance has a square in it because of Pythagoras. Kinetic energy ½mv² and spring potential ½kx² come from integrating Newton's and Hooke's laws — nobody chose to square, it is what the physics gives you. And most importantly, near any smooth minimum, Taylor's theorem guarantees that the function literally is quadratic, no matter how complicated the function looks globally. In all these cases, there is no choosing. The square is there.
  • It is a modeling choice when you are writing down a cost for fitting, tracking, penalizing, or weighting. You could penalize a mistake by |mistake|, or mistake⁴, or something stranger. Picking squared is a decision — one made because squared is differentiable, has closed-form solutions, matches the "small misses don't matter, big misses do" intuition, corresponds to Gaussian-noise assumptions in statistics, and — not coincidentally — matches what the function locally looks like near the optimum anyway. Alternatives (L¹ for robustness to outliers, Huber loss for a blend, L∞ for worst-case design) exist and are sometimes preferred.

Putting it together: near the minimum of anything smooth, power 2 is nature. Everywhere else — fitting, control, risk, design penalties — power 2 is a choice, but a choice that lines up so cleanly with the nature answer, and with the math, and with solvability, that it has become the overwhelming default. The class of problems called "quadratic programming" is the human-made decision to study exactly this well-behaved family.

Quadratic programming (QP) keeps the linear constraints of LP but allows the objective to be a quadratic function. That one change unlocks a large class of problems LP cannot handle cleanly.

Key idea. A QP is an LP whose objective is allowed to curve. Specifically, the objective is ½ xTQx + pTx — a quadratic bowl — and the constraints are the same linear inequalities we had before.

2 · Anatomy of a QP

The textbook way to write a QP uses compact matrix notation:

minimize    ½ · xT Q x  +  pT x
subject to   A x ≤ b
            x ≥ 0   (optional)
Reading the formula — what is each symbol?

That expression is terse. Before the symbols become intimidating, here is what every piece means in plain language.

  • x is the list of numbers you are choosing — the decision variables. With two variables it is simply x = (x₁, x₂); with n variables, x = (x₁, x₂, …, xₙ). The small ₁, ₂, ₙ are index labels, not exponents — "the first variable," "the second variable," and so on.
  • xT is read "x transpose." The small raised T is not a power — it does not mean "x to the power T." It is a linear-algebra notation that says "write the same list of numbers sideways, as a row instead of a column," so that the next matrix operation has matching dimensions. For understanding what is going on, you can simply read xT as "x, again."
  • xT Q x — three symbols side by side — is not ordinary multiplication of three numbers. The writing is a shorthand. With two variables it expands into the concrete sum
    Q₁₁·x₁² + Q₂₂·x₂² + 2·Q₁₂·x₁·x₂
    — the squares of each variable, plus a cross term. This sum is the curved part (the "bowl") of the cost.
  • pT x is the ordinary linear sum p₁·x₁ + p₂·x₂ + … + pₙ·xₙ — the same linear combination you used in LP.
  • Q is a square grid of numbers — a matrix — that sets the shape of the bowl (how stretched, how tilted). p is a list of numbers that tilts the bowl off-centre.
  • A x ≤ b is a compact way to bundle every constraint. Each row of the matrix A holds the coefficients of one inequality, and the matching entry of the vector b holds that inequality's right-hand limit. So one row of "A x ≤ b" is exactly a₁·x₁ + a₂·x₂ + … ≤ b — the same kind of linear rule you saw in LP.
  • The "x"s sitting next to other symbols (as in A x or Q x) are not multiplications of two numbers. They are matrix–vector products — a bookkeeping operation that amounts to "multiply each row of the matrix element-by-element with x, and add." With actual numbers, it collapses to ordinary addition and multiplication, as you will see below.

So if you have only two variables, that compact formula becomes this very concrete statement:

minimize    ½ · (Q₁₁·x₁² + Q₂₂·x₂² + 2·Q₁₂·x₁·x₂)  +  p₁·x₁ + p₂·x₂
subject to   a₁·x₁ + a₂·x₂ ≤ b   (one such line per constraint)
            x₁, x₂ ≥ 0  (if required)

Nothing mysterious: a polygon of allowed points (the linear inequalities), and an objective built from squared terms, a cross-term, and linear terms. The matrix notation above is just a way to write this same thing for any number of variables without running out of space.

Back to the matrix picture: the matrix Q controls the bowl's shape via its eigenvalues. When every eigenvalue is non-negative (positive semi-definite), the bowl opens upward and the QP is convex — easy to solve, one clear minimum. We will only consider convex QPs in this lecture.

Non-convex QPs (some negative eigenvalues → saddle-shaped objective) also exist and are much harder. They do not appear in most engineering applications and we will set them aside.

3 · Same region, different objective

This is the single most useful picture for understanding the LP→QP jump. The feasible region does not change — it is still a polygon defined by linear inequalities. What changes is the shape of the level sets of the objective you are optimizing.

  • LP: level sets are straight lines, the optimum sits at a corner.
  • QP: level sets are ellipses, the optimum can sit anywhere — inside the region, on an edge, or at a corner.

4 · Four real QPs

Markowitz portfolio

Choose weights x across assets to minimize portfolio variance (quadratic in weights through the covariance matrix), subject to a target expected return and position limits. This is the textbook QP of finance.

Ridge / constrained regression

Fit coefficients to data by minimizing squared error (quadratic in coefficients), subject to bounds or monotonicity constraints. Any squared-loss fit with linear constraints is a QP.

Support vector machines

Find the hyperplane separating two classes with the largest margin. "Margin" turns out to be 1/‖w‖, so maximizing it is minimizing ‖w‖² — a QP. This is what underlies classical SVM classifiers.

Model-predictive control

At every time step a robot or power grid solves a QP: minimize a quadratic cost on tracking error and control effort, subject to linear constraints on actuator limits. Done in milliseconds, millions of times a day.

The common thread: a quadratic penalty is the natural way to say "bigger deviations hurt more than smaller ones." Variance, squared error, tracking error, effort — all quadratic.

5 · How QPs are solved

In LP, the optimum lived at a vertex, so we hunted vertices. In QP, the optimum can be anywhere inside, so we use continuous methods. The most visual one is projected gradient descent:

  1. Take a small step in the direction of steepest descent (down the bowl).
  2. If the step leaves the feasible region, project it back onto the closest feasible point.
  3. Repeat until barely moving.

Production QP solvers use more sophisticated methods — active-set and interior-point algorithms — but projected gradient descent captures the essential idea and is what you would implement in a first pass. For our purposes today, "go downhill, stay inside" is the right mental model.

6 · LP vs QP at a glance

Linear Programming
  • Linear objective
  • Level sets: parallel lines
  • Optimum: at a vertex
  • Solvers: Simplex, interior-point
  • Applications: planning, scheduling, flow
Quadratic Programming
  • Quadratic objective (convex)
  • Level sets: concentric ellipses
  • Optimum: anywhere feasible
  • Solvers: active-set, interior-point, projected gradient
  • Applications: finance, regression, SVM, control

Everything to the left of both — the constraints, the idea of a feasible region, the notion of an optimum — is shared. The objective is what distinguishes them, and that single change drives everything downstream: where the optimum lives, which algorithm you use, which applications it fits.

7 · Takeaways

  • QP = linear constraints + convex quadratic objective.
  • Level sets are ellipses, so the optimum is not stuck at a corner.
  • If the unconstrained minimum lies inside the feasible region, that is the solution. Otherwise it sits on a face or corner where the gradient is balanced by active constraints.
  • Go-to algorithms: projected gradient (visual), active-set (classic), interior-point (large-scale). You will almost never write one yourself — reach for a library.
  • If your cost is "squared error," "variance," "tracking error," or "effort squared" with linear rules, you have a QP.
